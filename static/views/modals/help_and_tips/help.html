Help and Tips

Manager Tip: The mission of HKB Goals is to improve the performance of your team and increase your site's revenue. When employees have a system for setting and tracking goals and staying in peak state throughout the day, and when managers have a system that allows them to coach efficiently and provide timely feedback to staff, then performance improves and revenues increase.

Manager Tip: Motivation is a private, internal process that endows employees with the energy and direction needed to be engaged, happy, and successful. It differs from influence, which is an interpersonal process that often goes by names such as persuasion, compliance, and leadership. HKB Goals is a motivational tool, designed to energize and empower employees, and propel them towards self-actualization and fulfilling their potential.

Goal Tips

  • A goal is something you are striving to accomplish. It is a future-focused cognitive representation of a desired state that guides behavior. Goal setting and goal striving is the process of deliberately creating a discrepancy between your present state (where you are now) and your ideal state (your goal), and then working to minimize and eliminate that discrepancy.

  • Your goals should be difficult - but not impossible - to achieve. Difficult goals have a large discrepancy between the present state and the ideal state and will inspire you to work harder and longer. Easy goals do not provide enough discrepancy between your present state and your ideal state. If the discrepancy is small, then there is little motivation to resolve it.

  • Difficult goals energize and sustain behavior. Achieving difficult goals will generate feelings of pride, confidence, satisfaction, and competence. These positive emotions and their underlying neurochemicals (dopamine, serotonin, and endorphins) will generate success momentum and energize you further, helping you to reach future goals.

  • Set specific goals. Specific goals will keep you focused and on task. Specific goals (and your progress towards them) can be easily measured.

  • Connect your short-term goals to long-term goals whenever possible. Short-term goals provide frequent opportunities for feedback and positive reinforcement. Goal setting translates into increased performance only when there is timely feedback that documents your progress in relation to a specific goal. When you connect a Day goal to a longer-term goal, you endow that Day goal with a sense of purpose it otherwise would not have.

  • Your desire for personal growth is the ultimate motivational force. So tap into it! Set goals that are genuine, authentic, and self-concordant. These goals will help you to sustain effort and put you on the path towards self-actualization, towards fulfilling your potential and becoming the person you want to be.

  • You should have an appropriate balance of intrinsic (or mastery) goals and extrinsic (or performance) goals. Intrinsic goals pertain to: personal growth and self-actualization, relationships with others (such as family, friends, and co-workers), contribution to society, and physical and mental health. Extrinsic goals pertain to: financial success, demonstrating competence relative to others, social recognition, image, and status.

  • People whose goal is to approach success (“My goal is to be #1 for the month.”) outperform people whose goal is to avoid failure ("My goal is not to get written up.”). The type of goal you set for yourself and how you frame it can affect the quality of your motivation and your performance. Avoidance goals can lead to decreased self-esteem and feelings of incompetency. Set approach goals whenever possible, and eliminate avoidance goals from your mindset. Be positive!

  • Be careful about sharing your goals with people other than your manager. Some subscribe to the theory that sharing goals will help you achieve them because it creates social expectation and accountability. However, cognitive science says otherwise. When you share a goal with someone else, your mind is often tricked into feeling that the goal is already accomplished. If you must share your goals, then be sure you derive no satisfaction from doing so. A simple rule of thumb: Don’t share your goals, share your accomplishments!

Image Tips

  • Use images to provide you with the psychological nutrients needed to stay in peak state throughout the day. To be in peak state, we need to feel like we’re good at what we do; we need to feel competent. We need to feel like we’re in control of our environment and activities; we need to feel autonomous. And because we are inherently social animals, we also need to feel connected and loved.

Manager Tip: You can help your team stay in peak state by creating a work-environment that promotes and cultivates these three psychological nutrients: competency, autonomy, and relatedness.

  • Pictures of your past significant achievements help you re-live those positive experiences. Happy memories will help stimulate production of serotonin, the neurotransmitter responsible for feelings of competence, confidence, and well-being.

  • Pictures of family, friends, and pets trigger feelings of love and relatedness. Relatedness is the core psychological need for close emotional bonds and caring, reciprocal relationships. Relatedness and its underlying hormone, oxytocin, is one of the fundamental psychological nutrients necessary to achieve peak state and feelings overall well-being.

  • Studies designed to test the advice to “visualize success” determined that focusing on the goal itself actually interfered with goal attainment. Focusing on how to accomplish the goal, rather than the outcome, facilitated goal attainment. So to help generate more productive action towards your goals, use images that pertain to processes rather than outcomes.

  • Images that make you smile will help put you into peak state. Feel-good brain chemicals serotonin, dopamine, and endorphins are released whenever you smile. People who smile are generally viewed as more attractive, reliable, and sincere than people who don’t. So smile!

Rewards Tips

  • Be thoughtful in your approach to rewards as they have the potential to be very powerful drivers of motivation and goal achievement. A reward should be meaningful, desirable, and redeemable. Anticipation of receiving a reward stimulates dopamine production which regulates motivation and encourages action. Vary your rewards to ensure there is sufficient anticipation. The reward, the anticipation, and the dopamine release are key mechanisms for translating motivation into goal-directed action.

Manager Tip: The immediacy at which a reward is delivered partly determines its effectiveness. For example, if you are going to offer a SPIF, the sooner you pay it out, the more effective it will be, the higher its ROI.

Manager Tip: Beware of the “hidden costs” of rewards. An extrinsic reward applied to an intrinsically interesting activity will undermine future intrinsic motivation. Super-motivation does not occur. So use rewards only for tasks that have lower intrinsic interest but high social importance.
